Tokyo Ghoul

Based on Sui Ishida's manga, Tokyo Ghoul tells the story of a world where ghouls, beings who eat humans, exist not as monsters, but as humans as well.

Context

Tokyo Ghoul has some similarities to the popular Japanese novel by Osamu Dazai, No Longer human. College student Kaneki Ken under extremely unfortunate conditions becomes an artifical half ghoul after having a deceased ghoul's organs transplanted in him. His struggle through accepting that he can only eat humans for nourishment is an embittering one, but he also finds company and family within the pacifist ghoul group, Anteiku.'

Backlash

There is a lot of backlash concerning the anime, most of which argues that the anime is a poor adaptation of Ishida's manga. This is most certainly a valid point, as the manga fleshes out many important details while the anime, especially the second season leaves the watcher confused at points and events appear unnecessarily rushed, not allowing for the buildup of tension leading to Ishida's iconic tragedies. However, as someone who watched the anime first and then read the manga, I find high entertainment value in both. In particular, the anime vocalizes and visualizes some stunning scenes, in particular the last episode of the first season which contains the torture scene of Kaneki and a brilliant symbolic display using red spider lilies and white carnation. The Crux of the Show (as according to me)

I admit that a lot of dark anime do question humanity and in turn force you to question your faith in humanity. Tokyo Ghoul is not different. What does it mean to be human? Does it mean anything at all? Other anime like Parasyte, Shiki, and even Death Note force your to reconsider human nature in similar ways. However, the difference between other shows and Tokyo Ghoul is that Tokyo Ghoul is more a tragedy than it is a moral lesson. It enjoys displaying the best in humans only for them to be torn to pieces by an unfair society. It acknowledges that both the human side and the ghoul side have loved ones and pits them against each other so that the audience sympathizes with both but knows the inevitable doom that will befall everyone. People are an incorrigible species, the show seems to say. The show grapples signficantly with disillusionment and the extent to which one can be pushed before they are drive crazy or reveal their true intentions (often when it is too late).
